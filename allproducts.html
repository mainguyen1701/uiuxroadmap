Design Leadership Vision — 2026

Every school deserves software
as good as the decisions it helps make.

Applicaa sits at some of the most consequential moments in a young person's education — when a family applies to a sixth form, when a school makes an offer, when a student is enrolled. The software that supports those moments should feel trustworthy, clear, and fast. Right now, in places, it does not.

This roadmap is the plan to change that. Not by redesigning everything at once — but by building a shared foundation, fixing the highest-friction areas first, then systematically raising the quality bar across every product in the Applicaa family. By June 2026, the experience of using Admissions+ or MAT+ should feel like one coherent product, not a collection of screens that grew independently over time.

North Star
"A school admin who knows Admissions+ can pick up any Applicaa product and feel at home within minutes — because the patterns, the language, and the logic are the same everywhere."
Where we are today
Components rebuilt differently on every screen
7 different blue colour values across the product
Status badges with no shared meaning (Upcoming = green in some screens)
Settings nav with no active state or logical grouping
13 products with no shared identity layer
Applications abandoned on long single-page forms
AI capabilities planned with no UX framework to support them
Where we will be by June 2026
One design system — one source of truth for every team
Consistent tokens, colour, type, and spacing across all screens
Settings reachable in under 2 clicks from anywhere
Application completion rate at 90% with step-by-step flow
All 13 products on a shared identity and accessibility standard
AI interactions with trust arc, confidence indicators, and human override
A team with the skills, processes, and tools to sustain this quality
The shifting role of design — Jenny Wen, Future of Design in the AI Era
The traditional design process is over. The execution partner era has begun.
Engineering changed first. AI agents now ship code faster than any team can review it. The question engineers are asking is: "How do we keep up with ourselves?" Design cannot be the bottleneck in that world. The designers who thrive are the ones who move with engineering — not ahead of it, waiting to hand off.
Old model
60–70% mocking and prototyping
Hand off designs, wait for build
Designer as gatekeeper
Weeks of perfect mocks before anything ships
Now — at Applicaa
30–40% prototyping — rest is execution partnership
Build HTML and Tailwind directly alongside engineers
Taste, judgment, and decision accountability
Ship, learn, and iterate — not wait for perfection
Open question we are answering with this roadmap: In a world where AI can generate interfaces and engineers ship without waiting, who is accountable for product decisions? Answer: the designer. Judgment, taste, and the decision about what gets built — that is the irreplaceable role. This roadmap is that accountability made visible.
Four design principles guiding every decision
01
Consistency over cleverness
Familiar patterns that school staff learn once and apply everywhere. Novelty is a cost — every bespoke interaction is something a new user has to relearn.
02
Clarity at critical moments
Offer rounds, enrolment decisions, and application submissions are high-stakes. The UI at these moments must be unmistakably clear — no ambiguity, no guessing.
03
Built for the whole school
From the full-time admissions lead to the bursary coordinator who logs in twice a year. Every screen is designed for the least experienced user without slowing down the expert.
04
Earn trust through every interaction
From import errors to AI recommendations — every system response must feel honest, human, and in control of the situation. Trust is built in the small moments.

Phase 0 — Shared Foundation

Establish the Admissions+ and MAT+ design foundation using the DS Lookbook — the single shared reference for how every component, pattern, colour, and interaction should look and behave. Every improvement in Phases 1 and 2 is built using this language.

What is the DS Lookbook?
The DS Lookbook at designsaplus.com/lookbook is the shared design system for Admissions+ and MAT+ — a living reference documenting every reusable component, shared pattern, and design token across both products. It is the source of truth for how things look and behave in these two products.

When a developer needs to build a new screen, they start here. When a designer proposes a change, they document it here first. When two screens look different, the Lookbook is the arbiter of which is correct.
🔍 UX thinking — how we approach Phase 0
Research
Heuristic audit of existing Admissions+ and MAT+ screens. Component inventory across every screen type. Identify where visual drift has accumulated and why.
Design
Gap analysis between current patterns and DS Lookbook standards. Token mapping — what exists, what conflicts, what is missing. Document everything in the Lookbook before changing anything live.
Validation
DS Lookbook reviewed with engineering leads. Each component entry peer-reviewed before publishing. Consistency check across 10 representative screens before Phase 1 begins.
🎯 Skill prep — what the team needs for Phase 0
DS documentation practice — writing component specs and usage rules
Design token workflows — naming, publishing, and maintaining a token system
Lookbook contribution — how to add, review, and update DS entries
What Phase 0 delivers
🧱
Component library
Admissions+ MAT+
Every reusable UI element — inputs, buttons, tables, cards, panels, navigation, badges, and feedback components — defined in one place with a live preview, available variants, and usage guidance. No more ad-hoc rebuilds or searching for the right example.
Every developer and designer works from the same reference. Components look and behave consistently across every screen.
🎨
Design tokens
Admissions+ MAT+
The colour palette, typography scale, spacing system, and status colour map — all named and published as shared tokens. Navy #263C91, Coral #EC634B, status colours (Upcoming = blue, Completed = green, Cancelled = red, Full = amber), and the full Inter type scale are all defined here.
Colour, type, and spacing are consistent across the product. A future rebrand or dark mode is a token change — not a product-wide rebuild.
📐
Shared patterns
Admissions+ MAT+
Common interaction patterns documented beyond individual components — how filtering works across list screens, how empty and loading states are handled, how record detail panels open and close, how status is communicated consistently. These are the decisions that, without a shared reference, each developer makes differently on every screen.
Staff experience the same behaviours on every screen. Learning one list means understanding all lists.

Phase 2 — Product Redesigns

Validated, AI-accelerated prototypes across two parallel tracks — Admissions+ and MAT+. Built using DS Lookbook components from Phase 0 and AI prototyping tools. Each prototype is tested with school staff before handoff to engineering. Each track ships independently.

🔍 UX thinking — how we approach Phase 2
Research
End-to-end journey mapping with school admissions staff and parents. Analysis of application drop-off points, support call transcripts, and offer round processing times. One research sprint per product track before design begins.
Design
Iterative prototyping — lo-fi to hi-fi per screen. Each redesign tested with real users before handoff to engineering. Design and build run in parallel sprints, not sequentially.
Validation
Usability testing with 5 school staff per feature (Admissions+ track) and 3 MAT coordinators (MAT+ track). Target: 90% application completion rate, measurable reduction in offer round processing time.
Research synthesis — NN/G 2025 · MIT study · Vercel AI SDK
AI compresses Phase 2 from months to weeks — if the DS foundation is solid.

Phase 4 — AI-Native UX

Engineering is building three AI capabilities in 2026. This phase is the UX design work that makes each one usable — the interfaces, patterns, and interaction models that allow school staff and families to work with AI confidently, override it when needed, and trust what it shows them. Design leads each stage before engineering ships.

🔍 UX thinking — how we approach Phase 4
Research
Contextual research with families completing applications — where anxiety is highest, where trust breaks, where the process stalls. Staff interviews on AI comfort levels and override confidence. Benchmark review of AI-native products in adjacent sectors.
Design

Research foundation — Nielsen Norman Group, 2025
AI is the third UI paradigm in 60 years. Conversational design is not a feature — it is a new interaction model.
For 60 years, interfaces were command-based — users told the computer what to do, one step at a time. AI introduces intent-based outcome specification: users tell the system what they want, and the system works out how. This is not a chat window bolted onto existing UI. It requires a completely different design approach — designing outcomes and constraints, not screens and flows.
1945 – 1964
Batch processing
Users submitted complete instructions and waited for output. No feedback. No adjustment. One shot.
1964 – 2022
Command-based GUI
Users issue commands, see results, adjust. This is the model all Admissions+ screens currently follow — including every screen in this roadmap.
2022 onwards — Applicaa Phase 4
Intent-based outcome specification
Users state what they want. The system interprets intent, takes action, and surfaces a result. The designer's job shifts from designing steps to designing outcomes — and the guardrails the AI must operate within.
Six conversational design principles guiding Phase 4
Be transparent about being AI
NN/G research shows users calibrate language and expectations when they know they are talking to AI. Hiding it destroys trust the moment they discover it. Every Applicaa AI interaction identifies itself and its confidence level upfront.
Offer structure, allow flexibility
Prepopulated options for common inputs (buttons, chips, suggested answers) reduce typing effort. Free text input allows deviation. Both must coexist — forcing users to type everything or click everything both fail. Design for the middle.
Progressive disclosure of complexity
Reveal complexity gradually. Start with what the user said they want. Show detail only when they need it. Do not present every option upfront — the AI should ask one question at a time and offer depth on demand.
Design for deviation
Chatbots fail when users deviate from linear flows (NN/G). Every conversational flow must have a designed recovery path — what happens when the AI does not understand, when the user changes direction, or when the answer is not in the expected format.
Outcome-oriented constraints over screen design
In generative UI, designers define what the AI must achieve and the guardrails it must operate within — not every screen element. The designer specifies the goal, the constraints, and the fallback. The AI generates the interface moment by moment.
Speed as the trust signal
NN/G research: the primary advantage of AI over human agents is speed. Every interaction should be designed to reach resolution faster than the current screen-based equivalent. If it is not faster, the AI has not earned its place in the product.
Sources: Nielsen Norman Group — "AI: First New UI Paradigm in 60 Years" and "Generative UI and Outcome-Oriented Design" (2025)
Shared AI design foundations — built before any stage ships
🎨
AI DS component set
All products
Before any AI feature ships, the shared interaction patterns are defined and documented in the DS Lookbook: AI assistant panel, confidence indicator (three levels), thinking state, human handoff card, and override interface. Every product team uses the same components.
Consistent AI personality and trust signals across Admissions+, MAT+, and the wider family. No team builds AI UI from scratch.
🔁
Confidence + override pattern
Admissions+ MAT+
The single most important AI UX design decision: how does the system show confidence, and how does a human override it? Three confidence levels (high 90%+, medium 60–89%, low below 60%) with a consistent visual language and a one-action override that feeds back into the AI.
Staff trust AI recommendations because they can see why and disagree when they want to. Every override improves the model.
↗️
Escalation pathway
All products
Every AI interaction has a designed escalation path: AI unsure — asks for clarification — hands to human — provides direct contact. The path from AI to person is never ambiguous. Designed before any AI feature ships.
No dead ends. Families and staff always know how to get a human. AI failure modes are designed, not discovered in production.
Stage 1 — Admissions Copilot UX  Month 3–9 · aligned to engineering delivery
Engineering is building an AI assistant that helps admissions officers — auto-summarising applications, flagging anomalies, suggesting actions, and answering natural language queries on admissions data. Our job: design the interfaces that make those capabilities usable and trustworthy for school staff.
🪟
AI assistant panel — in-product design
Admissions+
Design how the AI Copilot sits within the existing Admissions+ UI — as a side panel or inline assistant, not a separate tool. Placement, trigger, dismiss, expand. Works alongside the staff workflow without replacing it.
Admissions officers access AI help without leaving the screen they are working on. No context switch.
🚩
Anomaly flag UI
Admissions+
Design how anomalies are surfaced in the application list and record view — what a flag looks like, what information it shows, how staff dismiss or act on it. Must not create alert fatigue or be ignorable.
Staff spot unusual applications immediately. Flags are specific enough to act on without opening the full record.
🔎
Natural language query interface
Admissions+
Design the UX for asking the system questions in plain English — "How many applications are waiting for references?" The input, the response format, the follow-up, and the fallback when the query is unclear.
Admissions data is queryable by any staff member without needing to know which report to run. Reduces dependency on support and reporting tools.
Stage 2 — Application Reviewer UX  Month 6–15 · aligned to engineering delivery
Engineering is building an AI that reviews applications against school criteria, highlights missing information, and recommends decisions with confidence scores. Human approves or overrides. Our job: design the review interface so staff can approve, override, and audit AI decisions with confidence.
📋
AI review summary panel
Admissions+
Design how the AI's assessment of an application is presented to staff — what it checked, what it found, what it is recommending, and how confident it is. Must be scannable in under 30 seconds.
Staff understand the AI's reasoning at a glance. Review time per application reduced. No black-box decisions.
⚠️
Missing information highlights
Admissions+
Design how gaps in an application are surfaced — inline within the record, in a summary list, with severity indicated. Must distinguish between "missing but optional" and "missing and blocking." Staff can request the missing item in one action.
Incomplete applications are resolved faster. Schools chase fewer applicants for information they actually already have.
✅
Human approval and override interface
Admissions+
Design the approve/override flow — how staff confirm an AI recommendation or disagree with it, what reason they give, how that feeds back. Full audit trail visible from the application record. Governance-ready by design.
Every AI decision has a human sign-off. Overrides are recorded and improve the model. Audit trail satisfies compliance requirements.
Stage 3 — Predictive Enrolment Engine UX  Month 12–18 · aligned to engineering delivery
Engineering is building a system to forecast enrolment from pipeline data, identify at-risk applicants likely to withdraw, and optimise communication timing. Our job: design the dashboards, risk signals, and communication interfaces that turn predictions into action.
📊
Enrolment forecast dashboard
Admissions+ MAT+
Design the data visualisation surface for predicted enrolment numbers — by subject, year group, and intake cohort. Confidence ranges shown alongside predictions. Drill-down from trust-wide (MAT+) to individual school.
Curriculum and staffing decisions are made with data, not gut feel. Trust-wide view available to MAT coordinators without manual collation.
🔴
At-risk applicant signals
Admissions+
Design how the system surfaces applicants who are predicted to withdraw — what signals are shown, how urgency is indicated, and what action staff can take directly from the alert. Design must prevent both over-intervention and under-intervention.
Schools intervene earlier with at-risk applicants. Withdrawal rate reduced. Intake targets met with fewer last-minute offers.
📨
AI-timed communication interface
Admissions+
Design how staff review and approve AI-recommended communication timing — "Send a reminder to these 12 applicants on Tuesday at 10am." Must show the reasoning, allow edits, and confirm before sending. Human approves every batch.
Communication reaches applicants at the right moment without manual scheduling. Staff remain in control of every send.
