Design Leadership Vision — 2026

Design is what stands between
good AI and trusted AI.

Phases 0–3 build the foundation — one consistent, trustworthy product across 13 schools. Phase 4 deploys AI on top of it. The sequence matters: AI earns trust faster when the design already has it. Consistency by April. AI begins June.

North Star
"A school using any Applicaa product trusts it enough to let AI assist their most important decisions — because the design earned that trust first."
How this team works — Jenny Wen, Future of Design in the AI Era
Design as execution partner: building alongside engineering, not waiting to hand off.
Traditional model
60–70% mocking and prototyping
Hand off designs, wait for build
Designer as gatekeeper
Weeks of perfect mocks before anything ships
Now — at Applicaa
30–40% prototyping — rest is execution partnership
Build HTML and Tailwind directly alongside engineers
Taste, judgment, and decision accountability
Ship, learn, and iterate — not wait for perfection
Who is accountable for product decisions when AI generates options? The designer. Judgment, taste, and the call on what gets built — that is the irreplaceable role. This roadmap is that accountability made visible.
Four design principles guiding every decision
01
Consistency over cleverness
Familiar patterns that school staff learn once and apply everywhere. Novelty is a cost — every bespoke interaction is something a new user has to relearn.
02
Clarity at critical moments
Offer rounds, enrolment decisions, and application submissions are high-stakes. The UI at these moments must be unmistakably clear — no ambiguity, no guessing.
03
Built for the whole school
From the full-time admissions lead to the bursary coordinator who logs in twice a year. Every screen is designed for the least experienced user without slowing down the expert.
04
Earn trust through every interaction
From import errors to AI recommendations — every system response must feel honest, human, and in control of the situation. Trust is built in the small moments.

📐 Research foundation — NN/G 2025
AI is the third UI paradigm in 60 years.
Conversational design is not a feature — it is a new interaction model.
For 60 years, interfaces were command-based. AI introduces intent-based outcome specification — users state what they want, the system works out how. This requires designing outcomes and constraints, not screens and flows.
1945 – 1964
Batch processing
Users submitted complete instructions and waited for output. No feedback. No adjustment. One shot.
1964 – 2022
Command-based GUI
Users issue commands, see results, adjust. This is the model all Admissions+ screens currently follow — including every screen in this roadmap.
2022 onwards — Applicaa Phase 4
Intent-based outcome specification
Users state what they want. The system interprets intent and surfaces a result. The designer's job shifts from designing steps to designing outcomes — and the guardrails the AI must operate within.
Source: Nielsen Norman Group — "AI: First New UI Paradigm in 60 Years" (2025)
Research foundation — designing with AI for trust and empathy
AI cannot feel empathy. But the experience of being understood can be designed.
Five principles shaping every AI interaction — from a family opening an application to a coordinator approving a recommendation. Read before designing any Phase 4 screen.
1
Empathy is designed, not programmed
The feeling of being understood does not require the AI to understand. It requires the designer to understand — and encode that understanding into every interaction, state, and moment of friction.
How to execute
Run empathy mapping before designing any AI flow. Fill the Thinks quadrant first. For a family filling an application: "Am I doing this wrong? Will my child miss out because of me?"
Design reassurance into the idle state. If a user pauses for 30+ seconds, the AI offers a shortcut — not an apology.
Use progress signals that feel human. Not "Step 3 of 8" — "You're about halfway. The next section is short."
2
Trust with AI forms faster and breaks faster than with humans
NN/G research: users form their trust model of an AI in the first 3 interactions. A confident wrong answer in interaction 1 can end the relationship entirely.
How to execute
Design the first interaction as a trust demo, not a task. The Copilot's first action should be impressive and low-risk: "I've pre-filled your school name from last year's data. Does that look right?"
Never let AI be confident and wrong. Every AI output that could be incorrect must show a confidence indicator. If below 80% confident, the AI asks rather than states.
Design the recovery moment. When AI gets something wrong, the recovery interaction is more important than the error itself.
3
Users don't reach for AI even when it can help
NN/G diary study: only 7% of needs were addressed by AI — even though 62% could have been. The gap is not capability. It is visibility and trust.
How to execute
Make AI capabilities visible at the moment of need. When a staff member is on the application list, the Copilot shows: "Ask me anything about these applications." Contextual, present, specific.
Use suggested prompts to show possibility. Pre-populate 3 example questions relevant to the current screen. Users discover capability through examples, not documentation.
4
Empower, don't apologise
NN/G: "The goal of design is not to be nice to users, but to empower them." Every AI failure mode must be designed to move the user forward — not to make the product feel better about itself.
How to execute
Rewrite every AI error state as an action. "I couldn't understand that" becomes "Here are two ways I can help — which is closer to what you meant?"
Design the override as empowerment. When a staff member overrides an AI recommendation, it should feel like the human won. "Your call — updated." Then: "Should I factor this in next time?"
Sources: Nielsen Norman Group — "Sympathy vs. Empathy in UX", "What Could an Intelligent Assistant Do for You?", "Trustworthiness in Web Design" (2025)

DS Lookbook Connection — Phase 0 → Phase 2
The Phase 0 Lookbook is the design spec for Phase 2 AI prompts.
Phase 0 built the DS Lookbook: every component documented with live previews, variants, and usage guidance. Every design token named and published. Every shared pattern specified.

In Phase 2, that documentation is the context AI tools need to generate high-quality, on-brand prototypes. When prompting v0.dev or Cursor, the team references the Lookbook directly — component names, token values, and pattern descriptions become the prompt. The result: prototype quality that matches the product's visual language without manual screen-by-screen design.

The connection: Thorough Phase 0 documentation = faster, more accurate Phase 2 prototyping. Every entry added to the Lookbook in Phase 0 is an investment in Phase 2 speed. The two phases are directly linked — not sequential checkboxes.

For the design team: When writing Lookbook entries in Phase 0, write for a future AI reader as well as a human one. Clear component names, precise token descriptions, and explicit usage rules make better prompts — which make better prototypes. This is the compounding advantage the design system provides.
