Session Recording in a School Context

Evaluates the privacy risks of deploying session recording across Admissions+, MAT+, and ApplicaaOne. Required under UK GDPR Article 35 before any deployment.

Purpose and scope
Session recording captures mouse movements, clicks, scroll behaviour, navigation paths, and potentially form field interactions to support UX research and product improvement.
Why this assessment is required
Session recording in a school context involving student and staff data meets the threshold for a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) under UK GDPR Article 35. Processing involves children (under 18), sensitive educational data, and large-scale monitoring of individuals' behaviour. This assessment must be reviewed and signed off by the DPO before any deployment.

Legal basis for processing
School staff — Admissions+ and MAT+
Recommended basis: Legitimate Interests (UK GDPR Article 6(1)(f))

Applicaa's legitimate interest: improving product UX to reduce support burden, increase adoption, and fulfil contractual service obligations to schools.

Legitimate Interests Assessment:
✅ Purpose test — genuine business interest in product improvement
✅ Necessity test — more targeted than surveys alone for identifying UI friction at scale
✅ Balancing test — staff are adults using a work tool; permissible with mitigations

Consent is not recommended as sole basis — difficult to withdraw in a work context.
Students and parents — ApplicaaOne
Do not deploy session recording without explicit DPO sign-off.

ApplicaaOne is directly used by students aged 15–18. The ICO Children's Code (Age Appropriate Design Code) applies to services likely to be accessed by children.

Relevant standards that affect session recording:
⛔ Standard 7 — Data minimisation: hard to justify session recording of a student completing an application
⛔ Standard 6 — Default settings: must be opt-in, not opt-out
⛔ Standard 10 — No profiling of children without compelling reason
⛔ Standard 2 — Best interests of the child: product improvement is a business interest, not a child interest
Key risks and mitigations
Six risks assessed. Each includes mitigation steps and residual risk after mitigation.
Critical
Risk 1 — Form field capture of student PII
Session recording tools capture keystrokes in form fields by default. Admissions+ and ApplicaaOne forms contain student names, dates of birth, addresses, and UCAS personal identification numbers.
Mitigation: Enable PostHog input masking on all <input>, <textarea>, and <select> elements. Engineering to audit every form in recording scope before go-live. Automated CI test for unmasked inputs. Spot-check 20 sessions before expanding scope.
Residual risk: Low
High
Risk 2 — URL-embedded student identifiers
URLs may contain application IDs, student reference numbers, or school IDs in path or query string (e.g. /applications/12345/review). These are captured by default.
Mitigation: Audit all URL patterns in recorded flows. Replace sequential IDs with non-enumerable UUIDs. Configure PostHog URL sanitisation rules to strip known ID patterns. Do not record full application detail views.
Residual risk: Low–Medium
Medium
Risk 3 — Staff expectation of privacy
School staff using Admissions+ may not expect their screen interactions to be recorded. Discovery without prior disclosure could damage trust and trigger an ICO complaint.
Mitigation: Update Terms of Service and Privacy Notice in plain English before deployment. Notify school IT administrators and DPOs 30 days before enabling recording via in-product banner and email. Provide an opt-out process for institutions.
Residual risk: Low
Medium
Risk 4 — Data retention and deletion rights
Under UK GDPR Article 17, individuals have the right to erasure. Session recordings linked to a user ID must be deletable when a school terminates their contract or a staff member leaves.
Mitigation: Use PostHog person deletion API. Set automatic deletion at 12 months. Ensure account deletion triggers recording deletion. Document and test deletion process before go-live.
Residual risk: Low
High
Risk 5 — Sub-processor risk (PostHog Cloud)
If using PostHog Cloud, PostHog becomes a sub-processor. Applicaa must maintain a DPA with PostHog, list them in the sub-processor register, and notify schools of any change.
Mitigation: Self-host PostHog on Applicaa's own AWS infrastructure. This eliminates sub-processor risk entirely. PostHog's open source licence permits self-hosting at no cost.
Residual risk: None if self-hosted
Critical
Risk 6 — SEN and FSM data exposure
Recording screens that display Special Educational Needs (SEN) or Free School Meals (FSM) status constitutes special category data processing under UK GDPR Article 9 — a significantly higher legal bar than legitimate interests.
Mitigation: Explicitly exclude any screen rendering SEN/FSM/safeguarding data from recording scope. Engineering to review and tag all Article 9 screens before recording is enabled. Maintain a documented exclusion list reviewed annually.
Residual risk: Low if scoped correctly · Critical if not
