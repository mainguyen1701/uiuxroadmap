Shared Foundation

Before we can improve any individual product, we need to fix the inconsistencies that exist across all three today. Right now, common elements — buttons, forms, status labels, tables — look and behave differently depending on which screen you're on. This phase standardises those shared elements so that every improvement we make in Phases 1–5 feels consistent and professional.

Why first: Inconsistency makes products feel unpolished and untrustworthy. Every phase after this relies on a stable foundation.

All products
An "Upcoming" event is shown with a green label — the same colour used for "Completed" or "Success." Staff are reading the text to understand status rather than trusting the colour, which slows them down and creates errors.
Every status label uses a consistent, predictable colour across all three products. Upcoming = blue, Completed = green, Cancelled = red, Full = amber, Draft = grey. Staff can scan a list and understand it instantly.

All products

All four components are documented with usage guidelines, examples, and variants. New screens are built from the same reference — no more ad-hoc rebuilds, no more drift.

Quick Wins — All Products

High-impact improvements to the daily workflows school staff rely on most. Every item in this phase ships independently — no item depends on another, so any of them can go live as soon as it's ready. Focus areas: settings navigation, the four core processes staff repeat every day, and the filtering and navigation pain points they hit on every screen.

Why now: These are the things that slow staff down most today. They don't require a full product redesign — they need focused, targeted fixes that deliver visible improvement immediately.
10
improvements
Settings navigation — restructured and easier to find your way around
⚙️
Settings navigation — Admissions+
Admissions+
Current state (from audit): The settings area has 8 top-level sections and over 20 sub-pages. The structure has three problems:

1. Ungrouped items: "Enrolment Settings" and "Enrolment Navigator" sit as isolated items between grouped sections. They belong together and feel orphaned where they are.

2. Overloaded groups: "School Settings" contains 8 very different sub-pages — from managing staff accounts to setting up payment fees to writing FAQs. These have nothing in common but share a group. Staff can't predict where anything lives.

3. Hard to know where you are: The active page is only shown by changing the text colour. In a 20+ item list, this is easy to miss. There's no description of what each section controls, so staff have to click around to find what they need.

4. Mixed audiences in the same menu: IT administration items (security, integrations, address mapping) sit next to items school admin staff use daily (student homepage, FAQs). A school receptionist has to scroll past technical settings they can't access to reach the settings they need.
Settings are reorganised into four clear groups based on who uses them and how often: Form & Application (everything related to how applications look and work), School Administration (staff accounts, permissions, communications), Enrolment (post-offer process, payments, progression), and Advanced (integrations, address mapping, technical configuration). Each group has a short description. The active page is clearly highlighted. The same structure is applied to MAT+ and ApplicaaOne settings.
Reduces support tickets about "where do I find X" Faster onboarding for new staff Applies to all 3 products
Processes — the four core workflows staff repeat every day
📤
1. Data import and export
Admissions+MAT+
Imports run silently. When staff import student data from Bromcom, a CSV file, or an MIS system, the process starts — and then nothing happens on screen. Staff don't know if the import is still running, has completed successfully, or has failed. They navigate away and come back hoping to see a result. When imports do fail, the error message is technical and gives no guidance on how to fix it.

Exports have no history. Once an export is triggered, it downloads immediately. There is no record of what was exported, when, or in what format. If a file is lost or needs to be re-shared, the entire export has to be run again.
Imports: A progress bar shows the import is running, with a live count of how many records have been processed. A clear success or failure message appears when it's done. Any errors explain what went wrong and how to fix it — in plain language, not technical text. The date and time of the last successful import is always visible.

Exports: Staff choose their format before exporting (spreadsheet, PDF, or CSV). The last five exports are listed with a re-download link. Large exports that take more than 30 seconds send a download link by email so staff don't have to wait on the screen.
Eliminates "did my import work?" support tickets Reduces data re-work from failed silent imports
🎓
2. Offer and enrolment management
Admissions+

Scale and Polish

A platform-wide quality pass covering accessibility standards, mobile usability, visual consistency, and long-term maintainability. This phase ensures that the work done in Phases 0–3 holds up under scrutiny, meets legal requirements, and doesn't create technical debt that makes future updates harder.

Why it matters: UK schools are public sector. Accessibility compliance is a legal requirement, not a nice-to-have. Failing an accessibility audit risks losing procurement contracts.
5
items
♿
Accessibility audit — WCAG 2.1 AA
All products
UK public sector procurement increasingly requires suppliers to meet WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility standards. MATs are public sector organisations — Applicaa products handling student and parent data must meet these standards or risk being excluded from procurement processes.
A full accessibility audit across all three products covering: text contrast ratios, keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, form error announcements, focus management, and skip navigation links. All critical findings fixed. An accessibility statement published for each product.
📱
Mobile and tablet — full pass
All products
Phase 1 covered the most critical screens in Admissions+. MAT+ and ApplicaaOne haven't had a mobile review yet. UK schools are primarily BYOD — staff and students use iPads, Android tablets, and phones as their main devices.
The ten most-used screens per product tested on iPhone, iPad, and Android. Tables that don't fit small screens stack to card layout. Text is readable without zooming. Buttons are large enough to tap accurately. Tested on real devices.
✨
Motion and animation standards
All products
When panels slide in, dialogs open, and notifications appear, the speed and style of the animation varies across the product. Some transitions are instant, some are slow. This makes the interface feel unpolished and inconsistent — especially noticeable when moving between different screens.
Four standard animation speeds defined and used consistently across all transitions. Respects the operating system setting for users who prefer reduced motion (important for users with vestibular conditions).
📖
Design standards and contribution guide
All products
There is no documented process for how new interface elements are designed, reviewed, and added. This is how inconsistencies accumulated in the first place — every developer had a slightly different approach. Without a published standard, the problems fixed in Phases 0–3 will reappear.
A published guide covering: how to propose a new component, the naming conventions, what documentation is required before something ships, the accessibility checklist, and how pull requests are reviewed. An automated check flags any use of old legacy styles before they can be merged.
🌙
Dark mode — groundwork
All products
Admissions staff work evenings. Parents register late at night. School events happen at 7pm. The expectation of a dark mode option is growing across all professional software — and all three Applicaa products currently have white backgrounds only.
All colour values in the design system are converted to semantic names (e.g. "background primary" instead of a specific hex code). The light values are assigned now. Dark values are defined and ready to activate — but not shipped yet. When dark mode launches, it requires changing one configuration, not rebuilding the product.

Designing for AI uncertainty — how the AI communicates what it knows
📊
Confidence indicators
All products
When AI makes a recommendation — "accept this application," "this document is a transcript," "this grade meets the requirement" — staff and parents need to know how confident the AI is. Without this, AI recommendations feel like black boxes and are either blindly trusted or completely ignored.
High confidence (90%+) — "I'm confident this is correct"
Medium confidence (60–89%) — "Let me double-check this with you"
Low confidence (<60%) — "I need your help with this — can you clarify?"
🔍
Transparency in reasoning
All products
If AI recommends an offer decision or identifies an issue with an application, staff need to understand why — not just see the outcome. A recommendation with no reasoning will not be trusted. This is especially critical in education, where decisions affect young people's futures.
Three levels of reasoning transparency, shown depending on how much detail the user needs:
• Simple: "Processing your documents..."
• Detail: "Found transcript, checking grades against requirements..."
• Full reasoning: "John's GPA is 3.5. The minimum requirement is 3.0, so this meets the criteria because..."

Staff can always expand to see the full reasoning behind any AI decision.

AI-Powered Research Strategy
How we use AI tools to run faster, deeper user research without replacing the human insight at the centre of it. AI multiplies what's possible — it doesn't replace the need to talk to real people.
Weeks 1–4 — Understanding current pain points
  • Week 1: AI-assisted background research — review UK school admissions policies, application requirements, and common complaints to generate informed hypotheses before talking to anyone
  • Week 2: A survey sent to 500 parents — with questions that adapt based on each respondent's answers. AI analyses open-text responses as they come in.
  • Week 3: In-depth interviews with 20 parents — AI generates personalised interview guides for each participant, transcribes sessions, and flags emotional moments and key quotes in real time
  • Week 4: AI-assisted synthesis — themes identified across all responses, a parent journey map generated, a prioritised pain point list ready for design
Weeks 5–8 — Testing AI interaction concepts
  • Week 5: Design 10 different conversation styles for the AI assistant — formal, friendly, brief, detailed. Use AI personas to test them before involving real users.
  • Week 6: AI personas test conversation prototypes — identifying confusion points, cultural insensitivities, and places where the conversation breaks down
  • Week 7: Real parent testing with 30 participants — compare how real parents respond against what the AI personas predicted. Refine based on differences.
  • Week 8: Final recommendations — which conversation approach works best for which type of parent. A testing framework for ongoing optimisation.
Weeks 9–12 — Trust and adoption
  • Week 9: Research how trust works in AI contexts across healthcare, finance, and education — apply the most relevant findings to our specific context
  • Week 10: Design and run trust experiments — A/B test different ways of explaining AI decisions and measuring which approaches build the most confidence
  • Week 11: Test across different interaction modes — chat, voice, and email. Find out whether parents trust AI differently depending on how they interact with it.
  • Week 12: Trust framework — a practical guide for designing AI interactions that build rather than erode confidence. A dashboard for monitoring trust metrics over time.
